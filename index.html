Maze Generation with Binary Decision Diagrams

This is a toy application of binary decision diagrams (BDDs) in order to generate random mazes. Suppose we have a BDD representing the connectedness function of subgraphs of a 5 by 5 grid of rooms. Then, Knuth's algorithm C lets us count how many solutions exist. For our chosen BDD, each solution is a connected subgraph, that is, a selection of passages such that paths exist between all pairs of rooms. Knuth describes a simple procedure that uses information computed during algorithm C to generate random solutions of the BDD. Putting this all together lets us generate random mazes. Easy, right?

Funnily enough, the reason why it's simplest to suppose the existence of such a BDD explains why this is only a toy application. Manufacturing the connectedness BDD in the first place requires a substantial investment of effort. In fact, this effort required seems to explode horrifically in the size of the grid. The precession of random mazes of the 5 by 5 grid shown above is the largest grid I've been able to handle to date. I've generated a few Knuth-style BDD graphs using graphviz, see here for an example of a BDD for the size 3 by 3 grid, consisting of 38 beads, including the two sinks.
